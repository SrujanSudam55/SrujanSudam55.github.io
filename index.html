A real-feeling example

This is the pattern I see when teams say “we need AI”.

  • Week 1 A backlog is “too large”. A tool is bought to auto-triage and route work.
  • Month 2 The dashboard shows speed. The floor still feels chaotic. Senior people still get dragged into emergencies.
  • Month 6 The model gets blamed. But the real problem was never the routing. It was the fuzzy greenlight decision that no one owned on a bad day.

If your “improvement” lives only on a slide, it is not an improvement. It is a costume.

Framework preview

A lightweight way to force clarity before you build anything.

  • 1) Name the decision Not the process. Not the tool. The decision that changes outcomes.
  • 2) Define the “challenge line” When should the case move forward, and when must it be challenged.
  • 3) Map accountability Who is responsible when the machine is wrong, or when the human ignores it.
  • 4) Choose the smallest useful automation Automate the boring parts. Keep the judgment visible.

If you want, I can turn this into a one-page worksheet you can use in stakeholder conversations.
